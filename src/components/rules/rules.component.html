Castling

This move allows for your king and rook to switch places under certain conditions. The king and rook both must have not moved yet. The king is not allowed to castle while in check, or if the king would land in check after castling. When castling, the king will move two spaces in the direction of the rook it is castling with, and the rook will be placed on the opposite side of the king.

En Passant

This special move means "in passing" in French. It is only allowed if the opponent moves their pawn forward two spaces. If your pawn and the opponents are next to each other at this moment, you can capture their pawn. Your pawn will move diagonally to the space behind the opponents, and their pawn will be captured.

Check

The King is placed in check whenever the opponents piece is able to capture the king at its current position. The player is required to either move their king out of check, block the check with another piece, or attack the piece that is placing the king into check. Also, a player is not allowed to move their king into a check position.

Checkmate

The only way to win in chess is by putting your opponent’s king in “checkmate.” Checkmate happens when the king is in “check” and has no moves to escape. The attacking piece cannot be captured, the threat can’t be blocked, and the king has no safe square. In the illustration, the white queen has the black king in check. Every escape square is covered by the queen, and the king can’t capture her due to the knight’s protection. The black bishop also can’t block the threat. This is checkmate.

Stalemate

A “stalemate” is a tie in chess, occurring when a player has no legal moves but isn’t in check. In this example, it’s white’s turn. The king has no safe moves, and the pawn is blocked, so the game ends in a stalemate. If any white piece had a legal move, the game would continue.
